Atmospheric Temperature

Mathematically describing the observed temperature patterns of Earth's atmosphere.
next

Below is a plot of the Earth's atmospheric temperature. Two temperature inversions are highlighted, defining the atmospheric layers: stratosphere and thermosphere. But why does this pattern occur? Furthermore, why does the temperature decrease in the troposphere and mesosphere? This post will outline the physical causes for these observations.

The Barometric Law

First, we need to derive the barometric law which can describe how certain physical attributes, such as pressure and density, change with altitude, z. For this, we define the rate of change of pressure as a function for p multiplied by gravitational constant, g (1). For the p-function will use the ideal gas equation (2).

$$ {\frac{dp}{dz} = -gp\quad (1)}\qquad\qquad{p = \frac{\rho k_B T}{\mu m_p}\quad (2)}\\$$

By assuming T, u and g as constant with respect to altitude, we can substitute (1) into (2) and integrate with respect to z giving (3).

$$ {p(z) = p(0)exp\left(-\frac{z}{H}\right)\quad (3)}\qquad\qquad{H = \frac{k_B T}{g \mu m_p}\quad (4)}\\$$

H is defined as the atmospheric scale height (8.5km for Earth). The above equation shows there is an exponential decrease in pressure with z and as pressure is proportional to density (ref. ideal gas equation) we can find the barometric law:

$$ {\rho(z) = \rho(0)exp \left(-\frac{z}{H} \right)\quad (5)}\\$$
Estimating a planet's surface temperature

It is possible to estimate an exoplanet's surface temperature. To do so, we start with measuring the radiation emitted by their host star. By assuming that the star and planet are in radiative equilibrium, we find that the planet's equilibrium temperature is related to the star's effective temperature by the following equation, where AB is the bond albedo, a measure of how reflective a surface is; Rstar is the star's radius and r is the distance from the star to the planet.

$$ {T_{eq} = \left(\frac{1-A_B}{4}\right)^{\frac{1}{4}}\left(\frac{R_{star}}{r}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}\qquad (6)}\\$$
Click for derivation!

On the right is the plot of Planck's Function representing 3 bodies with different temperatures. It describes the intensity of the electromagnetic radiation emitted by each body and is described by (6.1).

$${I(\lambda) = \frac{2 \pi h c^2}{\lambda^5}\frac{1}{exp\left(\frac{hc}{\lambda k_B T}\right)-1}\qquad(6.1)}\\$$

This equation is for bodies that act similarly to black bodies such as stars. A black body is an object that absorbs all incident radiation upon it and then re-emits it as a spectrum based on the temperature of the body.

(6.1) has then been integrated to find (6.2) where we have defined the relationship between temperature and radiation intensity by the Stefan-Boltzmann Law with the Stefan-Boltzmann constant:

$${I = \sigma_{SB}T^4\qquad(6.2)}\\$$

Before going any further, we need to define two important terms...

T(eff) Effective temperature, the temperature of a black body that emits the same total intensity as an emitting body.

T(eq) Equilbrium temperature, the temperature of a planet assuming its it radiative equilbrium with its host star.

Now we need to define luminosity, L and intensity at a distance from a star, S, as:

$${I_{star} = \sigma_{SB}T_{eff}^4}\qquad{L_{star} = 4\pi R_{star}^2 I_{star}}\qquad{S_{star} = \frac{L_{star}}{4\pi r^2}}\qquad(6.3,.4,.5)\\$$

The intensity re-emitted by a planet is dependent on a few things. First, it will reflect a fraction of the incoming radition (AB) and so will absorb 1-AB. Also, the radiation is only incident on the cross sectional area of the planet, but will emit radiation over its' entire surface area, so we account for that with (6.6). Putting this altogether gives (6.7).

$${\frac{\pi R_p^2}{4\pi R_p^2}}\qquad(6.6)\qquad\qquad{(1-A_B)\frac{S_{star}}{4} = I_p = \sigma_{SB}T_{eq}^4}\qquad(6.7)\\$$

We can then substitute (6.5) in to (6.7) to obtain our final equation...

$$ {T_{eq} = \left(\frac{1-A_B}{4}\right)^{\frac{1}{4}}\left(\frac{R_{star}}{r}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}}\\$$

While (6) can be a good approximation for a planet's effective temperature, is can be significantly different to its' surface temperature due to greenhouse gases in its' atmosphere. Gases such as C02, H2O and CH4 strongly absorb infrared radiation. Using Wien's displacement law (7), we can see that at terrestrial temperatures (~230K), a planet's emittance spectra will peak at infrared wavelengths. Overall this means that a large portion of outgoing radiation is absorbed by the atmosphere which warms the planetary surface.

$${\lambda_{max} = \frac{2.9 \times 10^{-3}mK^{-1}}{T} = \frac{2.9 \times 10^{-3}mK^{-1}}{230} \approx 10\mu m \qquad(7)}\\$$
A simple greenhouse model

On the left shows a simple greenhouse model. The atmosphere is transparent to visible light, so all radiation that is not reflected (1-AB) is transmitted to the planet's surface. The heated surface will emit infrared radiation. IR is absorbed by the atmosphere which subsequently re-emits it - both upwards and downwards.

The goal of this section is get an equation for surface temperature. To start with, we state that both the atmosphere and planetary surface are in radiative equilbrium. We can then equate the following...

$${(1-A_B)\frac{S_{star}}{4} = \sigma_{SB}T_{at}^4 = \sigma_{SB}T_{eq}^4}\\$$ $${(1-A_B)\frac{S_{star}}{4} + \sigma_{SB}T_{at}^4 = \sigma_{SB}T_{eq}^4 + \sigma_{SB}T_{at}^4 = \sigma_{SB}T_{s}^4}\\$$

Now we can define our equation for surface temperature in terms of the equilibrium temperature! The table below shows the outputs using measured values for bond albedo and distance from the sun for the planets Earth, Venus and Mars. The table also compares the calculated values for surface temperature with the actual value for each of the planets. Why do they vary?

$${T_{eq} = \left(\frac{1-A_B}{4}\right)^{\frac{1}{4}}\left(\frac{R_{star}}{r}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}}\qquad (6) \qquad \qquad{T_s = 2^{\frac{1}{4}}T_{eq}} \qquad (8)\\$$

For Earth, the calculated value is slightly warmer than expected. This suggests that the atmosphere absorbs <100% of the surface's emitted IR radiation. In comparison, Venus is estimated to be much colder than it actually is. This suggests that Venus has a much more massive atmosphere that further warms the surface. Finally, Mars' calculated value is quite a bit colder than we would expect. This suggests that its' atmosphere is thinner and absorbs less IR radiation. All of these inferences turn out to be correct!
