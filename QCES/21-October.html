Permafrost thaw

One of my lecturers mentioned permafrost this week and how its stability is so important in high latitude areas. Permafrost is just land that is permanently frozen and is predominantly found in Arctic regions such as Greenland, Canada, Alaska and Russia.

These areas are at risk of thawing due to rises in global temperatures - scientists predict that there will be widespread thawing by 2100 – and this could have various serious consequences, such as...

Flooding
Erosion
Diseases
Land destabilisation
Feedback Loops

Feedback loops are important systems to understand, and the warming of areas with permafrost is a good example of one. These areas cover around 25% of the Northern Hemisphere land masses and of all the organic carbon stored in soil globally, they hold around 50% of it!

As it thaws, microbes can begin to break down the organic matter - this releases both carbon dioxide and methane into the atmosphere which is where the feedback loop is created; these greenhouse gases lead to further warming and subsequently further melting. So basically as temperatures rise, the permafrost will continue to melt, meaning the knock-on effects will only become more prevalent.

Flooding, erosion and land destabilisation have the net effect of migration and relocation of Arctic communities. As the frost thaws, soils are more susceptible to erosion meaning shorelines recede and settlements become prone to flooding. Also, the melting of sea ice means that coastal areas are less protected against ocean storms.

Not only that but the cyclical thawing and freezing from annual temperature variations means that soil will shrink and expand. This means that overlying buildings and infrastructure will become incredibly unstable.

Some areas of Alaska have experienced erosion rates greater than 20 meters a year - for example, around 300 former residents of the village Newtok have had to abandon their homes and relocate elsewhere.

The arctic animals and marine life are also being affected by the changes in temperature; walruses are finding it difficult to secure food due to lack of sea ice and fish migrating northwards while polar bears struggle to find enough snow to build dens to give birth in.

Think of permafrost as sort of the glue that holds the northern landscape together
Another interesting phenomenon...

One interesting phenomenon I read about was the permafrost craters in Siberia! As of 2020, 17 of these craters that had been discovered since 2014 – they’re massive holes up to 70m wide and 50m deep that have unusually high levels of methane concentrations surrounding them. Looking at satellite imagery, researchers were able to observe the areas prior to the craters being formed. They found that mounds were forming and at some point an explosion took place where matter flew up to 300m away from the centre.

The only explanation for such an explosion was a pressure build-up of gas beneath the ground – assumed to be methane due to the high concentrations around the craters. It was only recently that researchers understood the process behind this build-up. A 2024 study described the process that is outlined in the diagrams below...

One component of the Siberian geology that is relatively unique, is its layers of methane hydrates and cryopegs. These structures come in pairs which are interspersed throughout the permafrost layer.

Methane hydrates are methane molecules trapped within a cage of water molecules - they likely originate when organic matter breaks down. Cryopegs are unfrozen, salty waters that are kept liquid due to the pressure and salinity - they lay atop the methane hydrates.

As shown in the diagrams, when permafrost begins to warm, meltwater makes its way down to the cryopegs, causing them to expand – thereby building up pressure. This pressure results in cracks in soil and the rapid depressurization that comes with the cracking causes the hydrates to decompose and release their methane gases – in a sudden, violent explosion.
