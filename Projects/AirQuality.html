Project Set Up
Since the 1990s there has been a push towards improving the natural ventilation of buildings. A well ventilated building is necessary to reduce build-up of harmful indoor air pollutants and excess moisture; poor ventilation has been shown to reduce both productivity of quality of life (Hou, J. et al. 2021, Apte, M.G. et al. 2000).

For this project, I was given a low cost air quality monitor that recorded various atmospheric concentrations such as CO2, VOC and PM10, where readings were taken every 5 minutes. To begin with, I just left the monitor running for a few days to see if anything interesting showed in the data.

I found that overnight while I was asleep, the CO2 concentrations seemed to level out – suggesting a steady state had been reached between my respiration rate and the room’s ventilation rate. What was interesting was that the steady state occurred at different CO2 levels each night.
Why is this?
I hypothesised that the reason for the variations in steady-state levels were to do with external, environmental conditions, such as wind speed, outdoor temperature or humidity. I found a paper (Linden, P.F. 1999) that backed this up – stating ventilation is mainly driven by wind speed– and so I decided to see if I could demonstrate a link between wind speed and my room’s ventilation rate.

The Air Change Rate (ACR) of a room can be calculated using just CO2 concentrations and is equivalent to ventilation rate. I found another paper (Batterman, S. 2017) that defined equations to find the ACR of room in three different scenarios...
1. Build Up
1 occupant enters the room after being outside long enough for the concentration to almost reach atmospheric concentrations.
2. Steady State
1 occupant asleep in the room, CO2 concentrations level out for over one hour (varies by less than 50ppm).
3. Decay
After a period of high CO2 concentration the occupant leaves the room with the window closed.
